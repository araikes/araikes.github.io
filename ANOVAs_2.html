Gender, Race, and BMI

There are multiple levels of Race in the dataset. Therefore, we cannot easily use a t-test. An ANOVA will allow us to simultaneously assess contributions of the racial groups relative to the contributions of individuals.

Gender, Race, and BMI

Here’s our general form

\(\hat{Weight}_{ij} = \mu + {Race}_i + \epsilon_j\)

where \(\mu\) is the grand average, \({Race}_i\) is the effect of the i^th race classification and \(\epsilon_j\) is the residual.

Gender, Race, and BMI

Before we look at the results of our ANOVA, we need to evaluate our assumptions. Recall that we assume:

  • Random sampling from a defined population
  • Independent observations
  • Homogeneity of variances
  • The residuals (\(\epsilon\)s) are approximately normally distributed
  • Equal sample sizes

Gender, Race, and BMI

We can evaluate the distributional and homogeneity assumptions graphically. First we need to run our ANOVA and store it in an R object.

race.aov <- aov(Weight ~ Race1, data = NHANES)

Gender, Race, and BMI

Next we need to plot various aspects of this. Luckily, R has a built-in for this. This code will let us view the hetero-/homoscedasticity of the residuals

plot(race.aov, 1)

Post-Hoc tests

Post-hoc tests allow us to identify where the differences lie when we have a significant F-test. There are numerous tests out there. Some are very good, some not so good. All of them do multiple pairwise comparisons (multiple T-tests). All of them penalize our \(\alpha\) level, so that the overall \(\alpha = 0.05\) but individual pairwise comparisons require a much smaller p-value to indicate significance.

Gender, Race, and BMI

We will use Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference test (HSD)

A Two-Way ANOVA

We can also assess whether weight differs by Race and Gender. The general form is:

\(\hat{Weight}_{ijk} = \mu + {Race}_i + {Gender}_j + {Race*Gender}_{ij} + \epsilon_k\)

A Two-Way ANOVA

twoway.aov <- aov(Weight ~ Race1*Gender, data = NHANES)
