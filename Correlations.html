Purpose

The purpose of this slideshow is introduce the concept of correlations and to discuss how they function both mathematically and visually.

Correlation

A correlation describes the linear relationship between two continuous variables. In practical terms, the correlation between two variables reflects the degree to which variables systematically change with each other.

A Visual Example to Start

Covariance

The starting point for computing a correlation is to compute the covariance. Like variance, covariance is a unitless measurement that describes how points in a scatterplot are distributed around the joint mean.

\[COV_{xy} = s_{xy} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{n - 1}\]

Covariance

Some notes about covariance:

  • If two variables are independent, then \(s_{xy} = 0\).
  • If \(s_{xy} = 0\), the two variables are not necessarily independent.
  • When there is a relationship, \(s_{xy} \neq 0\) and the magnitude of the value reflects the scale of the measurements being made, not the magnitude of the relationship.
  • The sign of \(s_{xy}\) describes the direction of the relationship.

Covariance example

# Original covariance
cov(rt.data$Height, rt.data$Weight)
## [1] 92.82561
# Scaling the values to metric
cov((rt.data$Height* 2.54), (rt.data$Weight * 0.454))
## [1] 107.0428

Correlation

Because covariance depends on the original scale of the data, it is hard to interpret whether we have observed a weak or strong relationship in the data. Covariance can really only tell you if there is a relationship. However, correlation provides a means for interpretting.

\[cor_{xy} = r = \frac{s_{xy}}{s_xs_y}\]

Correlation

By dividing by the product of the standard deviations, we make the our correlation coefficient scale-invariant (the scale of the values no longer matters). Correlations have useful properties:

  • The range of correlations is \(-1 \leq r \leq 1\).
  • \(r = 0\) indicates that there is no linear relationship in the data.
  • The sign of \(r\) indicates the direction of the relationship.
  • The absolute value of the magnitude of \(r\) indicates the strength of the relationship. Generally 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7 are the breakpoints for small, moderate, and strong relationships.
